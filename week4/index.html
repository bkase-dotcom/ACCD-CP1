Low-Poly Plant – Project Documentation

Blender study of a fiddle leaf fig

Background

My previous 3D experience has mostly been in Autodesk Fusion, using parametric, engineering-oriented modeling. Most recently I used Fusion to develop hardware for a portable weighted piano keyboard. Blender, by contrast, is built around polygon mesh modeling and is better suited for artistic work. Starting this project required me to shift away from a parametric mindset and get comfortable with Blender’s less rigid but more expressive mesh workflow. After the initial learning curve, I came to appreciate the extra freedom Blender provides.

Inspiration

For the subject of the project I looked at plants in my living space. I began by trying to model a money tree, but eventually pivoted to a fiddle leaf fig. The fiddle leaf has a simpler main stem and larger leaves, which made it more manageable for a low-poly study. I kept the original pot from the money tree attempt since it was easier to model and I preferred its texture. Working from a single reference photo is limiting for high-fidelity modeling, but it works well for low-poly forms.

Process

Pot Modeling

The project began with the pot, modeled from a cylinder mesh. I scaled the base inward to create a tapered shape, added an inset at the top opening, extruded downward to form the inner wall, and then extruded the inner face outward to create space for the soil.

First Stem Attempt

My first approach to the stems used cylinder meshes. I extruded, scaled, rotated, and duplicated cylindrical segments to create a cluster of trunks similar to the money tree reference.

However, the cylindrical topology made branching difficult. I realized it would be hard to get clean, organic branch junctions with this approach, so I decided to rethink both the plant choice and the modeling strategy.

Bézier Curve Stem

I switched to the fiddle leaf fig reference and rebuilt the main stem using a Bézier curve. At first the curve is only a path in 3D space with no volume. Using the curve’s bevel controls, I added thickness to turn it into a pipe-like stalk, then adjusted individual control points and radii in Edit Mode to create more organic, tapered variation along the stem and branches.

Leaf Modeling

Leaves were modeled from a simple square plane. I extruded edges outward to rough in the silhouette, subdivided faces, and used proportional editing to push vertices until the overall shape and curvature matched the fiddle leaf fig reference. Additional tweaks added slight bends and asymmetry to keep the leaf from feeling too mechanical.

Procedural Shading & Materials

I used procedural shading to build an organic leaf material, adding subtle variation in color and roughness plus simplified vein details. The finished leaf was duplicated, translated, and rotated along the branches until each stem felt balanced and believable.

For the pot and soil I used Physically Based Rendering (PBR) materials downloaded from Poly Haven. These were appended into the project’s Blender file and applied to the respective meshes, giving the pot a worn stone look and the soil a more detailed surface.

Rendering

For final presentation I rendered the plant inside a studio-style bedroom HDRI environment using the Cycles render engine. The first render looked off because the plant floated without a visible shadow. To solve this, I placed a simple mesh plane beneath the pot and set it as a Shadow Catcher in the object visibility settings. This allowed the HDRI to remain as the visible background while the plane only contributed realistic contact shadows.
