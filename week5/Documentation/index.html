Ultra-Portable Piano: Project Documentation

Studio-style product visualization in Blender

Modeling & Asset Preparation

The piano prototype was initially designed and modeled in Fusion 360, where I focused on establishing the overall proportions, key geometry, and housing form using a parametric workflow. Fusion allowed me to quickly iterate on dimensions, edge fillets, and component relationships while keeping the design grounded in real-world scale and manufacturability. Once the core components were finalized, the model was exported from Fusion 360 and imported into Blender for visualization, material work, and scene building. After import, the geometry was cleaned and organized into logical objects and collections to make it easier to work with during lighting, animation, and rendering. To create the full-length keyboard, I duplicated and repeated the key modules directly in Blender, aligning them precisely to extend the instrument while maintaining consistent spacing and proportions. This approach allowed me to efficiently scale the design without reworking the original Fusion model, while also taking advantage of Blender’s non-destructive transformation tools. By combining Fusion 360 for precise parametric modeling and Blender for layout, duplication, and visualization, I was able to move quickly from a functional design model into a presentation-ready prototype suitable for documentation and cinematic rendering.

Concept & Shot Plan

My initial concept was to place the piano in a hotel room environment, using a desk or table to suggest portability and real-world travel use. As the project evolved, I pivoted away from this contextual setting toward a more abstract, expansive black studio space. This change allowed the piano to be presented with the visual language of a professional product launch, where form, proportion, and material take precedence over narrative context.

Process

Base Scene Setup

The project began by isolating the piano model in a minimal Blender scene. The world background was set to near-black to eliminate distractions and establish a controlled studio environment. This approach kept the scene lightweight and allowed for rapid iteration.

Geometry & Proportion Refinement

I adjusted the piano’s overall length and proportions to ensure it read clearly from low, elongated camera angles. Emphasizing the linear form of the keyboard was important for communicating scale and portability.

Interface Elements

Simple cylindrical geometry was added to represent physical controls such as knobs and buttons. At this stage, the focus was on silhouette and placement rather than detailed interaction design.

Table Asset Integration

A table asset downloaded from Poly Haven was appended into the scene to ground the piano and provide a sense of scale. The table was intentionally kept neutral so it would support the piano without drawing attention away from it.

Planned Camera Movement (Bezier Path)

Although the final submission focuses on still imagery, the scene was designed with a cinematic intro animation in mind. A Bézier curve was created to act as a camera path, starting close to the piano’s internal/control region, dollying outward to reveal the body, sweeping along the keys, and resolving in an overhead aerial shot. This planned motion influenced framing and layout decisions even when camera constraints were temporarily disabled for still renders.

Lighting & Rendering

The lighting setup followed a traditional studio photography approach using area lights: a primary key light to define form, a soft fill light to manage shadows, and a rim light to separate the piano from the dark background. While early look development was done in EEVEE for speed, the final hero image was rendered using the Cycles render engine for improved realism, softer shadows, and more accurate material response.
